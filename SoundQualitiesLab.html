Distance Attenuation Simulation

Sound intensity $I$ follows the **Inverse Square Law**: $I \propto \frac{1}{r^2}$. As you move further from a point source, the power spreads over the surface area of a sphere!

The Wave Adder (Decibel Combination)

Why does adding a $60 \text{ dB}$ sound and a $55 \text{ dB}$ sound **not** equal $115 \text{ dB}$? Because decibels are logarithmic! To add them, we must first convert dB back to physical intensity ($I$), combine them, and recalculate decibels.

Constructive In-Phase Addition
Amplitudes sum up perfectly: $A_{total} = A_1 + A_2$
Enabled

Mathematical Proof

1. Convert dB to Intensity ($I/I_0$):
$I_1 = 10^6 \cdot I_0$
$I_2 = 10^{5.5} \cdot I_0 \approx 3.16 \cdot 10^5 \cdot I_0$
2. Amplitude Synthesis (In-Phase):
Since $I \propto A^2$, we have $A \propto \sqrt{I}$
$A_{total} = \sqrt{I_1} + \sqrt{I_2}$
3. Calculate Combined Intensity & Decibel:
Combined Output = 63.6 dB

Even though energy levels jump significantly, human hearing perceives this as only a minor increase! This demonstrates the logarithmic nature of the ear.
