Activity 1: The Bending Pencil

When a pencil is submerged in water, it appears broken or thicker. Why?

Observation 1: When dipped vertically, the submerged portion appears thicker because the cylindrical glass of water acts like a magnifying lens.
Observation 2: When dipped at an angle, light rays coming from the pencil inside the water change direction as they cross into the air, making the pencil look cracked at the water surface.

Activity 2: The Disappearing Coin

Place a coin in a metallic dish and walk away until it is just out of sight over the rim. When your friend fills the dish with water, the coin magically appears again!

Empty Cup: The rays of light from the coin travel in a straight line but are blocked by the opaque lip of the vessel. The eye cannot see it.
Water Added: Light rays exit the water and bend *away* from the normal line (downwards toward the observer's eye). The eye traces these rays back in a straight line, making the coin appear raised!

Activity 3: Parallel Glass Slab Double Refraction

Observe light entering a glass slab and then emerging back into the air. Let's look at the mathematics of parallel interfaces.

First Refraction (N on PQ): Light moves from Air (rarer) to Glass (denser). The beam bends *towards* the normal normal line.
Second Refraction (M on RS): Light moves from Glass back to Air. The beam bends *away* from the normal line. Since the faces are parallel, the final emergent angle $e$ equals the incident angle $i$.

🌈 Newton's Prism Lab

White light is a mix of many colors of different wavelengths. As white light enters glass at an angle, each color bends at a slightly different angle because the refractive index is wavelength-dependent!

Red: Longer wavelength, bends less ($n \approx 1.51$)
Violet: Shorter wavelength, bends more ($n \approx 1.53$)
💡 Isaac Newton's Discovery He was the first to show that the prism didn't paint the rainbow, but simply split the preexisting colored components inside the white beam!

📜 The Physics Laws of Refraction

First Law of Refraction: The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal perpendicular at the point of incidence all lie in the exact same plane.
Second Law (Snell's Law): For a given pair of media, the ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence ($\sin i$) to the sine of the angle of refraction ($\sin r$) is constant:
\frac{\sin i}{\sin r} = \text{constant} = n
Where $n$ is the refractive index of the second medium with respect to the first medium.
Special Condition (Normal Incidence): A light ray incident along the perpendicular normal line ($i = 0^\circ$) proceeds straight forward into the new medium without bending ($r = 0^\circ$).
