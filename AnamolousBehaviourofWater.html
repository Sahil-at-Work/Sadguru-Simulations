Aquatic Life Survival

In cold regions during winters, when the atmosphere goes below freezing point, the top surface water cools to $4^\circ\text{C}$ first and sinks down due to high density. As cooling continues, water on top drops towards $0^\circ\text{C}$ forming ice. Since ice is less dense, it floats and insulates the deeper water layers, allowing aquatic life to survive below.

Winter pipe bursts

When water inside pipes cools below $4^\circ\text{C}$, it expands rather than contracting. Once it solidifies completely to ice, this anomalous expansion exerts massive outwards hydrostatic pressure, often rupturing plumbing pipes or weathering rocks in freezing environments.

Maximum Density Core

At exactly $4^\circ\text{C}$ ($3.98^\circ\text{C}$ precisely), water possesses its absolute highest density of $1.0000\text{ g/cm}^3$. No matter how much colder or warmer it gets, its density will always be lower than this peak, leading to structural separation in still water bodies.
