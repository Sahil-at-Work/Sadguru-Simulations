1. Balanced State (Static Equilibrium)

While the stone is in your palm, your muscles exert an upward Normal Force ($F_N$) that perfectly counteracts gravity ($F_g$). Since the net force is zero ($F_{net} = 0$), the velocity remains zero.

2. Free Fall Initiation

The instant you open your hand, the upward support ($F_N$) drops to exactly 0. Only the pull of Earth's gravity remains. The stone accelerates downwards under the net unbalanced force.

Academic Reference

Kinematics of Gravitational Free Fall

Free fall is defined as the motion of an object experiencing solely the acceleration due to gravity, with air resistance eliminated or balanced in a vacuum. Under standard conditions, initial velocity ($u$) is $0\text{ m/s}$, and linear acceleration ($a$) simplifies to gravitational acceleration ($g$).

1. Velocity-Time Relation
$$v = g \cdot t$$
Velocity scales linearly with elapsed time.
2. Position-Time Relation
$$s = \frac{1}{2}g \cdot t^2$$
Falling distance grows quadratically with time.
3. Velocity-Position Relation
$$v^2 = 2g \cdot s$$
Relates terminal velocity directly to drop distance.

The Reality of Atmospheric Drag & Friction

In our physical world, true undisturbed free fall is impossible outside of vacuum chambers. As an object drops, it collisionally interacts with atmospheric molecules. This friction produces a upward opposing force called **Drag Force ($F_d$)**, along with a constant buoyancy coefficient. Once the upward friction vector perfectly balances the downward weight of the falling payload ($F_d = F_g$), acceleration drops to zero, and the object travels at a constant speed known as its **Terminal Velocity**.
