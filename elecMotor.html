How Does an Electric Motor Turn?

An electric motor consists of a rectangular loop of copper wire placed between the opposite poles of magnets. When electric current flows through the loop, the magnetic field exerts forces on the branches AB and CD of the coil.

According to Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, because the current flows in opposite directions in the two parallel branches, the magnetic forces act in opposite directions (one branch is pushed down, while the other is pushed up), initiating an anticlockwise or clockwise rotation.

The Commutator Magic

If current flowed continuously in the same direction inside the loop, the forces would swap once the coil crossed the vertical axis, causing the motor to wobble and stop.

The split-ring commutator reverses the direction of current inside the loop every half-rotation, ensuring the torque acts in a constant direction. This allows the motor to maintain continuous, fluid rotation!
