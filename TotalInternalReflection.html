Curriculum Insight

Partial vs. Total Internal Reflection

When light waves travel from an optically denser medium (such as water or glass with a higher refractive index, $n_1$) to an optically rarer medium (like air with a lower refractive index, $n_2$), the physical boundary behaves like a gateway.

Because light travels faster in the rarer medium, the light ray bends away from the normal line (the perpendicular line to the boundary). Mathematically, this means the angle of refraction $r$ is always larger than the angle of incidence $i$.

Phase A: Partial Reflection

At small incident angles $i$, a major portion of the light refracts out into the rarer medium. However, a small portion of the wave is always reflected straight back into the denser medium. This is known as partial internal reflection.

Phase B: Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

As you increase the incident angle, the refraction angle eventually hits $90^\circ$—meaning the light skims perfectly along the surface boundary. This angle of incidence is called the Critical Angle ($i_c$). If the angle of incidence is made even larger, the refracted ray cannot exist, and 100% of the light gets reflected back inside.

Nature's Phenomenon

How Rainbows Form

A rainbow is not a physical object in a fixed location; rather, it is an optical phenomenon. It requires three distinct stages of physics to happen within billions of suspended rain droplets:

1

Refraction and Dispersion

Sunlight enters the top half of the spherical drop. Going from air to water, it bends. Because different wavelengths of light experience slightly different refractive indices (violet bends more than red), they split apart—a process called dispersion.

2

Internal Reflection

The split light beams strike the internal back wall of the water droplet. Due to the droplet's curve, these beams strike at angles larger than the critical angle of water ($\approx 48.6^\circ$). This leads to a beautiful total internal reflection, redirecting the light forward and down.

3

Second Refraction & Emergence

The dispersed rays exit the droplet through the lower hemisphere. Bending once again as they enter air, they emerge focused in a narrow cone centered around 40° (violet) and 42° (red) relative to the incoming sunlight.

Scientific Legacy

Dr. C.V. Raman & Light Scattering

To truly understand light is to appreciate the legacy of India's preeminent experimental physicist, Dr. C.V. Raman. Winning the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1930 for discovering that when light traverses a transparent material, some of the deflected light changes wavelength (known as the Raman Effect), he pioneered our modern molecular understanding of materials.

"Why is the Sky Blue?"

Dr. Raman was famously inspired to research optics while on a ship voyage across the Mediterranean Sea, looking at the mesmerizing, deep blue waters. His scientific curiosity questioned the prevailing theory of the time and led him to prove that both the sky's and sea's colors are primarily due to the molecular scattering of sunlight by particles and molecules, rather than simple reflection!
