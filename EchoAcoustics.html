The 0.1s Retentive Threshold

The human brain retains any sound for exactly 0.1 seconds. If the reflected sound bounces back faster than this, they blur together (reverberation). Only when distance is sufficient ($>17.2\text{m}$ at $22^\circ\text{C}$) can we hear them as distinct events!

Temperature Factor

Sound travels faster in warmer air because air molecules have more thermal kinetic energy to pass along compressions. At hot temperatures, the minimum distance needed to hear an echo increases!

Interactive Explorations 3

Acoustics in Nature & Medicine

The laws of reflection are not just for auditorium designs. Explore how species navigate, and how engineers harness acoustics to view pre-natal babies, shatter stones, or hunt sub-surface craft!

Ultrasonic (30k-150k Hz)

Bat Echolocation

Bats emit rapid pulses of ultrasonic frequencies. By timing how long the reflection takes to arrive, they can pinpoint mosquitoes in absolute darkness.

Subsonic (~100 Hz)

Dolphin Navigation

Dolphins rely on low frequency underwater clicks. They sense items up to sizes matching the sound's wavelength (≥1.4m) using an analog sensory pathway.

Shockwaves (High-Amp)

Acoustic Lithotripsy

Doctors project non-invasive acoustic pulses, focusing maximum pressure via parabolic lenses directly on kidney stones to shatter them into tiny grains.

SONAR Systems

SONAR & Geology

Ships measure time-lag to locate subsurface targets or chart deep-sea floor reliefs. Similarly, geologists mapping seismic sound waves can find oil.
