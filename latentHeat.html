Understanding Latent Heat Concepts

Whenever there is a change in state of a substance, heat is either absorbed or given out but there is no change in the temperature of the substance.

Latent heat of a substance is the quantity of heat required to change the state of unit mass of the substance without changing its temperature.

This fundamental thermodynamic relationship is represented as: Q = mL Where L is the latent heat constant of the substance (measured in J/kg).

Latent Heat of Fusion (Lf): Energy needed to convert a unit mass of substance from solid to liquid state at its melting point.

Latent Heat of Vaporization (Lv): Energy needed to convert a unit mass of substance from liquid to gas state at its boiling point.

Why is Lv much larger than Lf? Completely separating molecules into vapor breaks all existing intermolecular bonds, requiring vastly more energy than simply shifting standard solid atomic lattice bonds.
